## Introduction to Script Coverage

### What is Script Coverage?

Script coverage is a comprehensive analysis and written report of a screenplay, typically 2-4 pages in length, that serves as the film industry's primary evaluation tool for assessing scripts. It functions as a "report card" for screenplays, enabling busy executives, producers, and agents to quickly determine a script's quality and production potential without reading the entire screenplay.

### Purpose and Function

Coverage serves multiple critical functions in the film industry:

- **Filtering mechanism**: Studios and production companies receive thousands of scripts annually. Coverage helps filter submissions to identify promising material
- **Time-saving tool**: Executives can review coverage in 5-10 minutes versus 1-2 hours to read a full script
- **Objective analysis**: Provides structured, consistent evaluation criteria across all submissions
- **Decision support**: Informs acquisition, development, and production decisions
- **Archive documentation**: Creates a permanent record of evaluated material

### Key Stakeholders

Script coverage is used by:
- Production companies and film studios
- Literary agencies and management companies
- Independent producers
- Film festivals and screenplay competitions
- Development executives
- Screenwriters (when seeking professional feedback)

## Standard Coverage Report Format

### Core Components

A professional script coverage report consists of four primary sections:

#### 1. Header / Cover Page

The header contains essential metadata:

**Required Information:**
- Script title
- Author/screenwriter name
- Submission date
- Covered by (reader's name)
- Coverage date
- Genre (e.g., Drama, Thriller, Romantic Comedy, Horror, Action)
- Page count
- Draft status (First Draft, Revised Draft, Final Draft, etc.)
- Submitted by (agent, producer, direct submission)

**Optional Information:**
- Estimated budget range (Low, Medium, High)
- Time period/setting
- Locations (number and complexity)
- Number of main characters
- Comparable films ("like X meets Y")
- Rights status
- Attachments (director, actors, producers)

#### 2. Logline

A logline is a concise, compelling one-to-two sentence summary (typically 25-35 words) that captures the essence of the story.

**Essential Elements:**
- **Protagonist**: Who is the main character? (usually described by role/archetype, not name)
- **Action/Goal**: What does the protagonist want or need to accomplish?
- **Conflict**: What stands in their way?
- **Stakes**: What happens if they fail?

**Logline Formula Examples:**

Formula 1: "In a world where [SETTING], [PROTAGONIST] must [ACTION], or else [STAKES]."

Formula 2: "[PROTAGONIST DESCRIPTION] who after [INCITING INCIDENT], wants to/must/struggles to [OUTER GOAL] by [PLAN OF ACTION] before [DIRE CONSEQUENCES]."

**Example Loglines:**
- "A computer hacker learns from mysterious rebels about the true nature of his reality and his role in the war against its controllers." (The Matrix)
- "A determined woman works with a mercenary pilot to stop a mining company from destroying a moon's indigenous population and sacred lands." (Avatar)

**Best Practices:**
- Keep it to 1-2 sentences maximum
- Focus on the protagonist's journey
- Avoid character names unless universally known (e.g., Abraham Lincoln, John F. Kennedy)
- Highlight what makes the story unique
- Make it compelling enough to generate interest

#### 3. Synopsis

The synopsis is a detailed, objective summary of the screenplay's plot from beginning to end, typically 1-3 pages depending on coverage depth.

**Length Guidelines:**
- **Standard coverage**: 1-2 pages
- **In-depth coverage**: 2-3 pages
- **Competition/contest coverage**: Sometimes up to 4 pages
- **Quick coverage**: 6-8 sentences (rare)

**What to Include:**
- Opening setup and inciting incident
- Main characters and their goals/motivations
- Major plot points and story beats
- Key turning points (end of Act 1, midpoint, end of Act 2)
- Character arcs and development
- Climax and resolution
- Important subplots that impact the main story
- Thematic elements

**What to Avoid:**
- Minor details that don't advance the plot
- Excessive dialogue quotations
- Personal opinions (save for comments section)
- Leaving the ending ambiguous (always reveal the ending)
- Scene-by-scene breakdown (focus on major beats)

**Writing Approach:**
- Write in present tense
- Maintain an objective, neutral tone
- Focus on major story beats and character development
- Keep it brisk and forward-moving
- Include character motivations, not just actions
- Use efficient, descriptive language

#### 4. Comments / Analysis

The comments section (typically 1-3 pages) provides the reader's critical evaluation of the screenplay across multiple dimensions.

**Structure:**

**Opening Assessment:**
- Brief overall impression
- What works well
- Primary strengths

**Detailed Analysis by Category:**

1. **Concept/Premise**
   - Originality of the core idea
   - Market appeal and commercial potential
   - Execution of the concept

2. **Story/Plot**
   - Structure and pacing
   - Plot coherence and logic
   - Effectiveness of turning points
   - Story arc satisfaction

3. **Characters**
   - Protagonist strength and appeal
   - Character development and arcs
   - Supporting character depth
   - Antagonist effectiveness
   - Character distinctiveness

4. **Dialogue**
   - Naturalness and authenticity
   - Character voice differentiation
   - Subtext and depth
   - Avoidance of on-the-nose dialogue

5. **Structure/Pacing**
   - Three-act structure adherence
   - Scene transitions
   - Story momentum
   - Act breaks and turning points

6. **Technical Elements**
   - Writing quality and style
   - Formatting and presentation
   - Action description clarity
   - Page count appropriateness

**Problem Areas:**
- Specific weaknesses identified
- Structural issues
- Character problems
- Dialogue concerns
- Pacing or logic issues

**Market Considerations:**
- Target audience identification
- Commercial viability
- Genre conventions adherence
- Competitive landscape
- Casting potential
- Budget feasibility

**Closing Recommendation:**
- Summary of strengths and weaknesses
- Development potential
- Final recommendation justification

#### 5. Evaluation Grid / Rating Matrix

Most coverage includes a standardized rating grid for quick visual assessment.

**Standard Categories Rated:**

- **Premise/Concept**: Originality and appeal of the core idea
- **Story/Plot**: Structure, pacing, and narrative effectiveness
- **Characters**: Development, depth, and appeal
- **Dialogue**: Quality, naturalness, and distinctiveness
- **Structure**: Three-act adherence and pacing
- **Pacing**: Story momentum and engagement
- **Writing Style**: Technical quality and readability
- **Originality**: Uniqueness and freshness
- **Production Values**: Budget feasibility and practical considerations
- **Marketability**: Commercial potential and audience appeal

**Rating Scale Options:**

Most common: **Excellent | Good | Fair | Poor**

Alternative scales:
- **5-point**: Excellent | Very Good | Good | Fair | Poor
- **Numeric**: 1-10 or 1-5 scale
- **Letter grades**: A+, A, A-, B+, B, etc.

Some categories may be weighted more heavily than others. For example:
- **High weight**: Concept, Story, Characters
- **Medium weight**: Dialogue, Structure, Pacing
- **Lower weight**: Originality (important but not determinative)

#### 6. Overall Recommendation

Every coverage report concludes with one of three standard recommendations:

**PASS** - Do not pursue
**CONSIDER** - Has potential but needs work
**RECOMMEND** - Strongly endorse for production/acquisition

(See detailed breakdown in Rating Systems section below)

## Core Elements of Analysis

### 1. Premise and Concept

**What is Evaluated:**
- **Originality**: Is the concept fresh and unique, or derivative?
- **Hook**: Does the premise immediately grab attention?
- **Marketability**: Is there a clear audience for this concept?
- **Execution potential**: Can this idea be effectively translated to screen?
- **High concept vs. low concept**: Is it easily pitched or more nuanced?

**Key Questions:**
- What makes this story different from similar films?
- Is the premise sustainable for a feature-length film?
- Does the concept have franchise or sequel potential?
- How does it compare to recent successful films in this space?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Completely original or fresh take on familiar territory; immediately compelling; strong commercial appeal
- **Good**: Solid concept with some unique elements; clear market appeal
- **Fair**: Familiar premise with limited originality; modest commercial potential
- **Poor**: Derivative or unclear concept; limited market viability

### 2. Story and Structure

**What is Evaluated:**

**Three-Act Structure:**
- **Act 1 (Setup)**: Establishes world, characters, and inciting incident (typically pages 1-25)
  - Ordinary world
  - Inciting incident (around page 10-15)
  - First turning point / Act 1 break (around page 25-30)

- **Act 2 (Confrontation)**: Rising action, obstacles, and complications (typically pages 25-85)
  - Escalating conflict
  - Midpoint reversal or revelation (around page 55-60)
  - Second turning point / Act 2 break (around page 85-90)

- **Act 3 (Resolution)**: Climax and denouement (typically pages 85-110)
  - Final confrontation
  - Climax
  - Resolution

**Pacing:**
- Does the story move at an appropriate speed?
- Are there sections that drag or feel rushed?
- Is there proper balance between action and quieter moments?
- Do scenes have clear purpose and forward momentum?

**Plot Development:**
- Are plot points logical and well-motivated?
- Do events flow naturally from character decisions?
- Are there plot holes or logical inconsistencies?
- Is the story predictable or does it offer surprises?
- Are subplots integrated effectively?

**Conflict and Stakes:**
- Is there clear, compelling conflict?
- Do stakes escalate throughout?
- Are obstacles meaningful and challenging?
- Is there both external and internal conflict?

**Resolution:**
- Is the climax satisfying and earned?
- Are plot threads resolved appropriately?
- Does the ending feel organic or forced?
- Is there proper emotional catharsis?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Perfectly paced with compelling plot progression; innovative structure; strong act breaks; satisfying resolution
- **Good**: Solid structure with clear acts; good pacing with minor issues; effective plot development
- **Fair**: Structural problems present; pacing uneven; some plot logic issues; resolution adequate
- **Poor**: Significant structural flaws; poor pacing; plot holes; unsatisfying resolution

### 3. Character Development

**What is Evaluated:**

**Protagonist:**
- **Clear goals**: What does the character want?
- **Strong motivation**: Why do they want it?
- **Flaws and complexity**: Is the character three-dimensional?
- **Character arc**: How do they change throughout the story?
- **Relatability**: Can the audience connect with this character?
- **Active agency**: Does the protagonist drive the story or react to events?
- **Unique voice**: Does the character have distinctive personality and voice?

**Supporting Characters:**
- **Distinctiveness**: Does each character feel unique?
- **Purpose**: Do they serve the story and protagonist's journey?
- **Development**: Are they more than one-dimensional?
- **Relationships**: Are character dynamics compelling?

**Antagonist:**
- **Clear opposition**: Do they effectively challenge the protagonist?
- **Motivation**: Do they have understandable reasons for their actions?
- **Complexity**: Are they more than pure evil/obstacle?
- **Threat level**: Are they a worthy opponent?

**Character Consistency:**
- Do characters behave consistently with established traits?
- Are changes in character behavior properly motivated?
- Do characters avoid convenient personality shifts for plot purposes?

**Diversity and Representation:**
- Are characters diverse and representative?
- Are stereotypes avoided?
- Is diversity handled authentically?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Multi-dimensional protagonist with clear arc; memorable supporting cast; complex antagonist; all characters feel authentic and distinct
- **Good**: Strong protagonist with clear journey; solid supporting characters; effective antagonist
- **Fair**: Protagonist somewhat underdeveloped; supporting characters lack depth; antagonist functional but basic
- **Poor**: Flat, uninteresting characters; no meaningful arcs; stereotypical or inconsistent behavior

### 4. Dialogue

**What is Evaluated:**

**Naturalness:**
- Does dialogue sound like real human speech?
- Is it appropriate to character, setting, and time period?
- Does it avoid being overly expository or "on the nose"?

**Character Voice:**
- Does each character have a distinctive way of speaking?
- Can you identify who's speaking without reading the character name?
- Do speech patterns reflect background, education, personality?

**Subtext:**
- Do characters say what they mean or is there subtext?
- Are emotions conveyed through what's unsaid?
- Does dialogue have layers of meaning?

**Efficiency:**
- Is dialogue concise and purposeful?
- Does every line serve character, plot, or both?
- Are there unnecessary conversations or filler?

**Authenticity:**
- Does dialogue feel true to the genre?
- Is period-specific language used appropriately?
- Are dialects and accents handled well?

**Balance:**
- Is there appropriate balance between dialogue and action?
- Are there scenes that are too dialogue-heavy?
- Is visual storytelling utilized effectively?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Sharp, distinctive dialogue that reveals character; excellent subtext; perfectly natural; zero exposition dumps
- **Good**: Solid dialogue with mostly distinct voices; generally natural; occasional on-the-nose moments
- **Fair**: Functional dialogue with limited distinctiveness; some awkward or expository passages; inconsistent character voices
- **Poor**: Stilted, unnatural dialogue; all characters sound the same; heavy exposition; no subtext

### 5. Pacing and Momentum

**What is Evaluated:**

**Overall Pacing:**
- Does the story maintain engagement throughout?
- Are there sections that feel slow or rushed?
- Is there appropriate rhythm and variation?

**Scene Pacing:**
- Does each scene have clear beginning, middle, and end?
- Are scenes appropriately timed?
- Is there escalation within scenes?

**Narrative Drive:**
- Does the story compel the reader forward?
- Are there compelling questions that keep pages turning?
- Is tension maintained and escalated?

**Balance:**
- Is there proper balance between:
  - Action and quieter moments
  - Tension and release
  - Exposition and story progression
  - Character development and plot advancement

**Page Count:**
- Is the script an appropriate length (typically 90-120 pages)?
- Does it feel too long or too short for the story being told?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Perfect pacing that never drags; constant forward momentum; ideal length for the story
- **Good**: Generally well-paced with strong momentum; minor slow sections; appropriate length
- **Fair**: Uneven pacing with noticeable slow spots; some sections feel rushed or dragged; length issues
- **Poor**: Significant pacing problems; story drags or rushes; poor scene timing; inappropriate length

### 6. Writing Style and Technical Quality

**What is Evaluated:**

**Prose Quality:**
- Is action description clear and vivid?
- Is writing style appropriate to genre and tone?
- Are there spelling, grammar, or formatting errors?

**Visual Storytelling:**
- Does the writer show rather than tell?
- Are visual moments effectively described?
- Is cinematic potential evident on the page?

**Format and Presentation:**
- Does the script follow industry-standard formatting?
- Is it easy to read and professionally presented?
- Are scene headings, action, and dialogue properly formatted?

**Economy:**
- Is writing concise and efficient?
- Are action paragraphs kept to 3-4 lines maximum?
- Is purple prose avoided?

**Technical Proficiency:**
- Proper use of camera directions (sparingly)
- Appropriate scene transitions
- Correct dialogue formatting
- Proper parentheticals usage

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Exceptional writing quality; perfectly formatted; highly visual and cinematic; effortless read
- **Good**: Strong writing with clear visuals; properly formatted; professional presentation
- **Fair**: Adequate writing with some issues; minor formatting errors; occasionally unclear
- **Poor**: Weak writing quality; formatting problems; difficult to read; numerous technical errors

### 7. Originality and Creativity

**What is Evaluated:**

**Uniqueness:**
- Does the script offer something fresh and new?
- Are familiar elements approached in innovative ways?
- Is there a distinctive voice or vision?

**Genre Innovation:**
- Does it adhere to or subvert genre conventions?
- Are genre elements used creatively?
- Does it blend genres effectively?

**Tropes and Clichés:**
- Are overused story beats avoided?
- Are character archetypes given fresh treatment?
- Does dialogue avoid clichéd phrases?

**Creative Execution:**
- Are creative choices justified and effective?
- Is there evidence of unique perspective?
- Does the script take creative risks?

**Note**: Originality is often weighted less heavily than other categories, as execution matters more than pure novelty.

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Highly original with fresh approach to story and characters; innovative and memorable
- **Good**: Contains original elements while using some familiar story beats effectively
- **Fair**: Largely familiar material with limited original touches; relies on conventional approaches
- **Poor**: Derivative and clichéd; offers nothing new; feels like a copy of existing material

### 8. Production Values and Budget Considerations

**What is Evaluated:**

**Budget Implications:**
- **Low budget** (under $5M): Limited locations, small cast, minimal effects
- **Medium budget** ($5M-$50M): Multiple locations, moderate cast, some effects
- **High budget** (over $50M): Extensive locations, large cast, significant effects/spectacle

**Production Complexity:**
- Number and variety of locations
- Period settings requiring costumes/props/sets
- Special effects and CGI requirements
- Action sequences and stunts
- Number of cast members
- Weather/environmental factors
- Foreign locations

**Feasibility:**
- Can this be produced on a reasonable budget for its type?
- Are there production challenges that would make filming difficult?
- Does the scope match the likely budget tier?

**Casting Considerations:**
- Are there roles suitable for bankable stars?
- Is the cast size manageable?
- Are there age-appropriate roles for marketable actors?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Appropriate scope for budget; production-friendly while maintaining vision; strong casting potential
- **Good**: Reasonable production demands; some challenges but manageable; good roles for talent
- **Fair**: Potentially expensive or complex production; scope may exceed budget; limited casting appeal
- **Poor**: Unrealistic production demands; prohibitively expensive; major practical challenges

### 9. Marketability and Commercial Potential

**What is Evaluated:**

**Target Audience:**
- Who is the primary demographic for this film?
- Is the target audience clearly defined?
- Is the audience size substantial enough?

**Demographics typically considered:**
- Age (Kids, Teens, 18-34, 35-54, 55+)
- Gender (Male, Female, All)
- Geographic (Domestic, International)

**Genre Appeal:**
- Does it fit into a marketable genre?
- Is there demonstrated audience appetite for this genre?
- How has the genre performed recently at box office?

**Competitive Landscape:**
- How does this compare to similar recent films?
- What makes it stand out in its genre?
- Is the market saturated or underserved?

**Marketing Potential:**
- Are there clear, compelling marketing hooks?
- Is it easily pitchable?
- Does it have trailer-friendly moments?
- Is there merchandising potential?

**Distribution Considerations:**
- Theatrical potential vs. streaming
- International appeal
- Festival potential
- Awards potential

**Comparable Films ("Comps"):**
- What successful films is this similar to?
- Can it be positioned as "X meets Y"?

**Rating Criteria:**

- **Excellent**: Strong commercial potential with clear target audience; highly marketable; strong comps
- **Good**: Solid market appeal; identifiable audience; reasonable commercial prospects
- **Fair**: Limited commercial appeal; niche audience; challenging to market
- **Poor**: Minimal commercial potential; unclear audience; not marketable

### 10. Theme and Emotional Resonance

**What is Evaluated:**

**Thematic Clarity:**
- What is the script exploring thematically?
- Are themes effectively integrated into the story?
- Do themes emerge organically or feel forced?

**Common Themes:**
- Love and relationships
- Family and identity
- Good vs. evil
- Coming of age
- Redemption
- Justice and injustice
- Power and corruption
- Survival
- Freedom vs. control

**Emotional Impact:**
- Does the script engage emotionally?
- Are there genuine moments of emotion?
- Does the climax provide catharsis?
- Will audiences care about the outcome?

**Depth:**
- Does the script operate on multiple levels?
- Are there layers of meaning?
- Does it provoke thought beyond entertainment?

**Universal Appeal:**
- Do themes resonate universally?
- Can diverse audiences connect with the core ideas?

## Rating Systems and Terminology

### The Three-Tier Recommendation System

The cornerstone of script coverage is the final recommendation, which determines a script's fate within the production pipeline.

#### PASS

**Definition**: The script is not suitable for production or further development and should not be pursued.

**Percentage of scripts**: Approximately 95-98% of all submitted scripts receive this rating.

**Indicators for PASS:**
- Fundamental story problems that would require complete overhaul
- Weak or unclear premise
- Poor execution across multiple categories
- Significant structural flaws
- Underdeveloped or uninteresting characters
- Unnatural or problematic dialogue
- No clear market or audience
- Derivative material without fresh perspective
- Major technical or formatting issues
- Budget requirements that don't match commercial potential

**Coverage Language:**
- "This script is not ready for production"
- "Unfortunately, this material is not right for our company"
- "Despite some interesting elements, the script has too many fundamental issues"
- "I recommend passing on this project"

**Does PASS mean it's terrible?**
Not necessarily. Many PASS scripts are competently written but simply don't meet the specific needs of that particular company, lack commercial viability, or compete unfavorably with similar material already in development. The script may be well-crafted but not exceptional enough to stand out in a crowded marketplace.

**Can a PASS script be resubmitted?**
Generally, if a script receives a PASS at a company, it's archived and that company won't reconsider it unless significantly revised and resubmitted through proper channels (agent, manager, or requested resubmission).

#### CONSIDER

**Definition**: The script has significant potential and strong elements, but also notable problems that need to be resolved before it can be produced. It warrants further evaluation and possible development.

**Percentage of scripts**: Approximately 2-4% of submitted scripts.

**Indicators for CONSIDER:**
- Strong premise with solid execution in some areas
- Compelling characters but perhaps underdeveloped supporting cast
- Good structure but pacing issues
- Interesting concept that needs refinement
- Strong writing but story problems
- Marketable but needs work to maximize potential
- Good for a specific actor or director if attached
- Would work with significant rewrites
- Budget concerns that could be addressed
- Strong in most areas with 1-2 significant weaknesses

**Coverage Language:**
- "This script shows promise but needs development"
- "With revisions, this could be a strong project"
- "I recommend considering this for development"
- "The writer shows talent; worth further conversation"
- "Strong enough to warrant a meeting with the writer"

**What happens with CONSIDER scripts:**
- May warrant a meeting with the writer
- Often triggers a request for rewrites or a second draft
- Might be passed to higher-level executives for additional reads
- Could lead to development deal discussions
- May be held for potential packaging with specific talent
- Sometimes used to establish relationship with promising writer

**Variations:**
Some companies use expanded categories:
- **Strong Consider**: Very close to recommend; minimal changes needed
- **Consider**: Has potential but needs work
- **Consider with Reservations**: Interesting but significant problems present

#### RECOMMEND

**Definition**: The script is exceptional and ready for production, or very close to it. The reader strongly endorses pursuit of the material.

**Percentage of scripts**: Less than 1% (often 0.1-0.5%) of submitted scripts.

**Indicators for RECOMMEND:**
- Exceptional writing across all categories
- Compelling, original premise
- Outstanding characters with strong arcs
- Tight, effective structure
- Sharp, distinctive dialogue
- Strong commercial potential
- Clear target audience
- Appropriate production budget
- Competition for similar material likely
- Could attract A-list talent
- Minimal revision needed
- High confidence in success potential

**Coverage Language:**
- "I strongly recommend pursuing this project"
- "This is exceptional material that we should acquire immediately"
- "This script is ready for production"
- "Do not let this script go to another company"
- "This writer is exceptionally talented"

**What happens with RECOMMEND scripts:**
- Immediate attention from top executives
- Often triggers competitive bidding situation
- Fast-tracked for acquisition or development
- Writer meetings scheduled immediately
- Potential for pre-emptive offers
- May circulate to A-list directors and actors
- Often leads to deals within days or weeks

**Does RECOMMEND guarantee production?**
No. Even highly recommended scripts face numerous hurdles:
- Budget approval
- Casting challenges
- Rights issues
- Market timing
- Company priorities
- Competition from other projects

However, a RECOMMEND significantly increases a script's chances of being developed and potentially produced.

### Rating Grid Systems

Most coverage includes a standardized grid for quick visual assessment:

#### Four-Point Scale (Most Common)

**Excellent**: Exceptional quality; standout achievement
**Good**: Solid, professional work; above average
**Fair**: Adequate but unremarkable; below professional standard
**Poor**: Significantly flawed; unprofessional

#### Five-Point Scale

**Excellent**: 5/5
**Very Good**: 4/5
**Good**: 3/5
**Fair**: 2/5
**Poor**: 1/5

#### Numeric Scales

**10-point scale**: 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest)
**5-point scale**: 1 (lowest) to 5 (highest)

#### Letter Grades

**A+ to F scale**: Similar to academic grading
- A+/A: Excellent
- A-/B+/B: Good
- B-/C+/C: Fair
- C-/D/F: Poor

### Industry Terminology

**Above-the-line**: Creative talent costs (writers, directors, actors)

**Below-the-line**: Technical crew and production costs

**Beats**: Major story points or moments

**Coverage**: The report itself; also used as a verb ("to cover a script")

**Development hell**: When a project is in development limbo for extended period

**Heat**: Industry buzz or interest in material

**High concept**: Easily pitchable premise with broad commercial appeal

**Hook**: The compelling element that grabs attention

**IP (Intellectual Property)**: Copyrighted creative material

**Logline**: One-sentence story summary

**Notes**: Development feedback (different from coverage)

**On the nose**: Dialogue that states exactly what's meant without subtext

**Option**: Exclusive rights to purchase material for specified period

**Package**: Combination of script with attached talent (director, actors)

**Plothole**: Logical inconsistency in the story

**Reader**: Person who writes coverage (also: Story Analyst, Script Reader)

**Spec script**: Screenplay written without being commissioned

**Stakes**: What the protagonist stands to lose or gain

**Track**: Follow or monitor a script's progress through the industry

**Treatment**: Extended prose outline of a screenplay (5-30 pages)

## Types of Coverage

### 1. Studio/Production Company Coverage (Internal Coverage)

**Purpose**: Primary evaluation for acquisition and production decisions

**Audience**: Internal executives, producers, and development staff

**Key Characteristics:**
- Most formal and comprehensive
- Strictly confidential (not shared with writers)
- Used to determine whether to pursue material
- Focuses on commercial viability and production feasibility
- Influences major financial decisions
- Typically 3-4 pages total
- Includes budget and casting considerations

**Process:**
1. Script submitted to studio/production company
2. Assigned to story analyst/reader
3. Coverage written (typically 48-72 hours)
4. Filed in company database
5. Accessible to executives for decision-making
6. High-rated coverage triggers additional reads

**Turnaround Time**: 24 hours to 2 weeks, depending on priority

**Cost**: Internal (salaried readers) or contracted ($50-$150 per coverage)

**Readers**: Typically professional story analysts with industry experience

### 2. Agency/Management Coverage

**Purpose**: Evaluate material for potential representation

**Audience**: Agents and managers

**Key Characteristics:**
- Assesses writer's talent and material's saleability
- Focuses on client potential and marketability
- Determines whether to sign writer as client
- Evaluates whether to submit script to buyers
- Often includes market positioning strategy
- May assess writer's voice and potential across projects

**Coverage helps answer:**
- Should we represent this writer?
- Is this script marketable to our buyers?
- How should we position this material?
- What's the best strategy for this writer's career?

### 3. Contest/Competition Coverage

**Purpose**: Evaluate scripts for screenplay competitions

**Audience**: Competition judges and administrators

**Key Characteristics:**
- Focuses on writing craft and storytelling ability
- Less emphasis on commercial viability
- More emphasis on originality and voice
- Often uses numeric scoring systems
- Multiple readers per script (usually 2-3)
- Scores are averaged or compared
- May include developmental feedback for finalists

**Common Competitions:**
- Nicholl Fellowship (Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences)
- Austin Film Festival
- PAGE International Screenwriting Awards
- BlueCat Screenplay Competition
- TrackingB

**Scoring often includes:**
- Overall quality score (1-100 or 1-10)
- Category ratings (story, character, dialogue, etc.)
- Written comments
- Advancement recommendation (quarter-finalist, semi-finalist, finalist)

### 4. Development Notes (Script Notes)

**Purpose**: Provide detailed feedback to help writers improve their scripts

**Audience**: The screenwriter

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Length**: Significantly longer (4-15+ pages vs. 2-4 pages for coverage)
- **Tone**: Constructive and supportive, directly addressing the writer
- **Detail**: Scene-by-scene analysis; specific suggestions
- **Purpose**: Help fix problems, not just identify them
- **Solutions-oriented**: Offers options and alternatives
- **Personal**: Can be more subjective and creative
- **Collaborative**: Assumes development relationship

**Key Differences from Coverage:**

| Coverage | Development Notes |
|----------|-------------------|
| For executives (not writer) | For writer |
| Identifies problems | Identifies AND solves problems |
| 2-4 pages | 4-15+ pages |
| 72 hours turnaround | 5-7 days turnaround |
| Pass/Consider/Recommend | Developmental roadmap |
| Objective assessment | Collaborative improvement |
| Internal use only | Shared with writer |

**Development Notes Sections:**
- Overall impressions

- Act-by-act breakdown
- Scene-specific problems and solutions
- Character development suggestions

## Coverage in the Decision-Making Process

### The Script Acquisition Pipeline

Understanding how coverage fits into the broader decision-making process is crucial:

#### Stage 1: Submission

**How scripts arrive:**
- Agent/manager submission (preferred route)
- Producer or development executive referral
- Contest winner or finalist
- Direct query (unsolicited, often not read due to legal concerns)
- Industry connection or networking
- Commissioned work

**Initial filtering:**
- Many companies won't read unsolicited scripts
- Queries may receive fast coverage or form rejection
- Established relationship material gets priority

#### Stage 2: Coverage Assignment

**Who reads:**
- Junior story analysts or readers (entry-level)
- Freelance coverage services
- Story department staff
- Development executives (for priority submissions)

**Prioritization:**
- A-list writer or referral: Fast-tracked
- Agent submission from major agency: High priority
- Contest finalist: Moderate priority
- Query submission: Low priority or declined

**Timeline:**
- High priority: 24-48 hours
- Standard: 3-7 days
- Low priority: 1-4 weeks or never

#### Stage 3: Coverage Review

**Who reviews coverage:**
- Development executives
- Creative executives
- Producers
- Heads of production
- Sometimes legal and business affairs (for high-interest projects)

**Review process:**
- Quick scan of logline and recommendation
- If PASS: Archived, no further action
- If CONSIDER or RECOMMEND: Read synopsis and comments
- High ratings trigger additional reads

#### Stage 4: Decision Points

**PASS decisions:**
- Script archived in company database
- Writer receives form rejection or no response
- Material not reconsidered unless significantly revised
- No meetings or follow-up

**CONSIDER decisions:**
- May trigger second read by senior executive
- Possible writer meeting request
- Potential for development discussions
- Script may be "tracked" for writer's future work
- Could lead to rewrite opportunity
- Might be held for specific talent attachment

**RECOMMEND decisions:**
- Immediate escalation to senior executives
- Multiple reads by decision-makers
- Competitive situation assessment
- Financial analysis begins
- Writer meeting scheduled quickly
- Potential for option or purchase offer
- Market research and comparable film analysis
- Packaging discussions (director, actors)

#### Stage 5: Green Light Process (for pursued material)

Coverage is just the beginning. A script that passes coverage faces many more hurdles:

**Development Phase:**
1. **Option/Purchase**: Acquire rights to material
2. **Development deal**: Hire writer for rewrites
3. **Creative development**: Work with writer on revisions
4. **Budget analysis**: Detailed production budget created
5. **Market analysis**: Target audience and competitive landscape
6. **Packaging**: Attach director and/or actors

**Pre-Production Approval:**
7. **Studio approval**: Multiple executive levels must approve
8. **Financial approval**: Budget and financing secured
9. **Production timeline**: Schedule determined
10. **Green light**: Official approval to begin pre-production

**Statistics:**
- Of 100 scripts submitted: ~95 receive PASS
- Of 100 scripts submitted: ~4-5 receive CONSIDER
- Of 100 scripts submitted: ~0-1 receive RECOMMEND
- Of scripts that receive RECOMMEND: ~10-20% get optioned
- Of optioned scripts: ~5-10% make it to production
- Of scripts in production: ~80-90% are eventually released

**Development timeline:**
- Coverage to option decision: Days to months
- Option to green light: 1-3 years typical
- Green light to release: 2-4 years typical
- Total submission to theatrical release: 3-7 years average

### The Role of Multiple Reads

**Why multiple reads matter:**

Few major decisions are made on a single coverage report. Instead:

**First Read (Story Analyst):**
- Initial filter
- Identifies material worth further review
- Provides baseline assessment

**Second Read (Development Executive):**
- Confirms or contradicts first coverage
- Provides more experienced perspective
- Determines whether to pursue

**Third Read (Senior Executive/Producer):**
- Final decision authority
- Business and creative assessment
- Green light or pass decision

**Divergent Coverage:**

When readers disagree, several approaches are used:

1. **Additional reads**: Bring in more readers for tiebreaker
2. **Senior read**: Have high-level executive read directly
3. **Writer meeting**: Assess writer's talent and vision in person
4. **Revised submission**: Request rewrite and resubmit
5. **Conservative pass**: When in doubt, pass (risk-averse approach)

### Coverage Databases and Tracking

**Industry tools:**

- **Studio databases**: All coverage archived and searchable
- **Tracking boards**: Websites like Tracking Board, The Blacklist track industry material
- **Coverage sharing**: Some companies share coverage informally
- **Writer tracking**: Coverage helps track emerging writers
- **Material tracking**: Follow scripts across town and development stages

**Impact:**

- A PASS at one company is often visible to others
- High ratings create industry buzz and heat
- Coverage can make or break a writer's reputation
- Material can be tracked for years after initial coverage
- Writers' progression tracked across multiple scripts

## Writing Best Practices for Coverage

### For Coverage Readers and Analysts

#### 1. Logline Crafting

**Do:**
- Keep it to 1-2 sentences (25-35 words ideal)
- Focus on protagonist, goal, and conflict
- Include stakes
- Make it compelling and specific
- Capture the unique hook
- Use active voice

**Don't:**
- Include character names (unless universally known)
- Use vague descriptors
- Reveal the ending
- Include minor plot details
- Use questions as your logline
- Make it boring or generic

**Examples:**

**Weak**: "A man goes on a journey and faces challenges."

**Strong**: "A young hobbit must journey across Middle-earth to destroy a powerful ring before a dark lord uses it to enslave the world."

**Weak**: "Sarah discovers something about her life that changes everything."

**Strong**: "When a hacker discovers reality is a computer simulation, he joins a rebellion to free humanity from machine overlords."

#### 2. Synopsis Writing

**Structure:**

**Opening paragraph:**
- Introduce protagonist and world
- Establish tone and genre
- Present inciting incident

**Middle section:**
- Major plot developments
- Character arcs and relationships
- Key turning points
- Escalating conflict

**Final paragraph:**
- Climax
- Resolution
- Character outcomes

**Writing style:**

**Do:**
- Use present tense throughout
- Maintain objective, neutral tone
- Focus on major beats, not minutiae
- Include character motivations
- Reveal the ending (always!)
- Keep paragraphs brief (4-6 sentences)
- Maintain forward momentum
- Use character descriptions (not just names)

**Don't:**
- Include personal opinions (save for comments)
- Quote dialogue extensively
- Describe every scene
- Leave the reader in suspense about the ending
- Use future tense or past tense
- Include irrelevant subplots
- Make it dry and mechanical

**Pacing:**
- Act 1: 25-30% of synopsis
- Act 2: 40-50% of synopsis
- Act 3: 20-30% of synopsis

**Character introductions:**

**Good**: "JOHN (30s), a disillusioned detective haunted by his past..."

**Bad**: "John is introduced..."

#### 3. Comments Section

**Organization:**

**Opening (2-3 sentences):**
- Overall impression
- Recommendation foreshadowing
- Tone-setting

**Strengths (1-2 paragraphs):**
- What works well
- Standout elements
- Positive aspects

**Analysis by category (2-4 paragraphs):**
- Story/Structure
- Characters
- Dialogue
- Pacing
- Originality/Marketability

**Weaknesses (1-2 paragraphs):**
- Problems identified
- Specific examples
- Impact on overall quality

**Market considerations (1 paragraph):**
- Target audience
- Commercial potential
- Comparable films
- Budget considerations

**Conclusion (2-3 sentences):**
- Summary
- Final recommendation
- Confidence level

**Tone and language:**

**Do:**
- Be professional and respectful
- Be specific with examples
- Be honest but constructive
- Balance criticism with praise
- Use industry terminology appropriately
- Write clearly and concisely
- Support opinions with evidence

**Don't:**
- Be mean-spirited or personal
- Use overly academic language
- Be vague ("it was good" vs. "the dialogue crackles with wit")
- Contradict yourself
- Include spoilers without purpose
- Ramble or repeat yourself
- Let personal biases dominate

**Useful phrases:**

**Strengths:**
- "The script's greatest strength is..."
- "Particularly effective is..."
- "The writer demonstrates strong command of..."
- "A standout element is..."

**Weaknesses:**
- "The primary challenge is..."
- "The script would benefit from..."
- "Unfortunately, [element] undermines..."
- "A significant issue is..."

**Balance:**
- "While [positive], [negative]..."
- "Despite [weakness], [strength]..."
- "Although [element] works well, [other element] needs development..."

#### 4. Rating Grid Consistency

**Calibration:**

Ensure ratings align with comments and recommendation:

**For RECOMMEND scripts:**
- Most categories should be Excellent or Good
- No categories rated Poor
- Maybe 1-2 Fair ratings maximum

**For CONSIDER scripts:**
- Mix of Excellent, Good, and Fair
- Perhaps 1 Poor rating
- Average should be Good

**For PASS scripts:**
- Multiple Fair and Poor ratings
- Few if any Excellent ratings
- Average Fair or below

**Avoid:**
- Rating everything as Good (not useful)
- Ratings that contradict written comments
- All Excellent ratings (nothing is perfect)
- Inconsistency between categories and recommendation

#### 5. Objectivity and Professionalism

**Managing bias:**

Coverage should be as objective as possible, but complete objectivity is impossible. Be aware of:

**Genre bias**: Don't downgrade horror because you prefer drama
**Taste bias**: Separate personal taste from quality assessment
**Trend bias**: Don't chase current trends or dismiss them unfairly
**Comparison bias**: Each script on its own merits
**Writer bias**: Don't let writer's reputation influence coverage

**Professional standards:**

- Read the entire script before writing coverage
- Give every script a fair chance
- Meet deadlines consistently
- Maintain confidentiality
- Be thorough and careful
- Proofread your coverage
- Follow company guidelines
- Update knowledge of industry trends
- Understand current market conditions

### For Writers Receiving Coverage

#### Understanding Your Coverage

**Reading coverage strategically:**

1. **Start with recommendation**: Sets context for everything else
2. **Read logline**: Does it capture your story accurately?
3. **Scan synopsis**: Did the reader understand your story?
4. **Study ratings**: Where are the weaknesses?
5. **Analyze comments**: Specific problems to address
6. **Look for patterns**: Multiple mentions of same issue = priority

**Warning signs in coverage:**

- Reader misunderstood your plot (clarity problem in script)
- Logline doesn't match your intention (concept not clear)
- Synopsis has major errors (story confused or confusing)
- Multiple low ratings in same area (significant weakness)
- Reader found it predictable (not as original as you thought)

**Positive signs:**

- High ratings across the board
- Enthusiastic language in comments
- Specific praise for unique elements
- Reader engaged with characters and story
- Few or minor suggested improvements
- Strong recommendation

#### Acting on Coverage Feedback

**Prioritizing revisions:**

**High priority** (address first):
- Story structure problems
- Character development issues
- Major logic holes or plot problems
- Unclear protagonist goals or stakes
- Significant pacing issues

**Medium priority**:
- Dialogue improvements
- Supporting character development
- Scene-level pacing
- Tonal consistency
- Genre convention adherence

**Low priority**:
- Minor formatting issues
- Small dialogue tweaks
- Trimming descriptions
- Polish and style

**When to revise:**

- When multiple readers identify the same problems
- When coverage reveals fundamental misunderstandings of your intent
- When structural issues are identified
- When character problems are noted
- Before sending to additional companies

**When not to revise (yet):**

- Based on single coverage from service (get multiple opinions)
- When feedback contradicts your vision (unless multiple readers agree)
- When suggestions feel generic
- When issues noted are minor polish items

**Getting the most value:**

1. **Request clarification**: Many services offer follow-up questions
2. **Get multiple reads**: One opinion isn't enough
3. **Compare notes**: Look for consensus across coverages
4. **Consult peers**: Share coverage with trusted writer friends
5. **Test solutions**: Try suggested changes and see if they improve the script
6. **Iterate**: Coverage → Revise → New coverage

## Conclusion: The Central Role of Coverage

Script coverage is the fundamental filter and evaluation tool of the film and television industry. It serves multiple essential functions:

**For the Industry:**
- Efficient evaluation of vast material influx
- Standardized assessment criteria
- Risk mitigation for expensive investments
- Discovery of new talent and material
- Documentation and tracking
- Decision support infrastructure

**For Writers:**
- Professional feedback on craft
- Market reality check
- Development roadmap
- Industry validation
- Career development tool
- Submission readiness assessment

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **Coverage is the gatekeeper**: Nearly all material submitted to industry must pass through coverage
2. **Quality matters**: Less than 1% of scripts receive RECOMMEND
3. **It's not personal**: Coverage assesses material, not writers' worth
4. **Multiple reads matter**: One coverage isn't definitive
5. **Understand the system**: Knowledge of coverage improves both writing and strategy
6. **Invest wisely**: Professional coverage before submission prevents wasted opportunities
7. **Learn the language**: Understanding coverage terminology empowers better decisions
8. **It's a tool, not a verdict**: Coverage informs decisions but doesn't make them
9. **Context matters**: Coverage varies by purpose (studio vs. competition vs. services)
10. **Improvement is iterative**: Use coverage to improve, then get new coverage

**The Future of Coverage:**

Emerging trends:
- **AI-assisted coverage**: Tools analyzing scripts for patterns
- **Data-driven analysis**: Comparing scripts to successful comps
- **Faster turnaround**: Technology enabling quicker reads
- **Hybrid models**: Combining human and AI analysis
- **Transparency**: More writers accessing coverage insights
- **Specialization**: Genre-specific and niche coverage services
- **Global perspective**: International market considerations
- **Diversity focus**: Representation and inclusion analysis

However, human creativity assessment remains irreplaceable. Coverage will continue to evolve, but its core function—evaluating storytelling quality and commercial potential—remains constant.
